Confirmation of Polish Citizenship by Descent

The acquisition of Polish citizenship by descent is an intricate legal process governed by the foundational principle of jus sanguinis, ensuring that the right of blood transcends geographic boundaries and the passage of time. For the global Jewish diaspora, particularly those whose ancestors emigrated during the tumultuous decades of the 20th century, the process is not one of naturalization but rather a formal confirmation of a pre‑existing legal status.

This procedure, legally designated as the confirmation of possession or loss of Polish citizenship, requires an exhaustive reconstruction of family lineage and a rigorous analysis of the shifting legislative landscape from 1920 to the present day. The pursuit of a Polish passport represents more than the acquisition of a travel document; it is a restoration of a historical link to a nation‑state that serves as a gateway to the European Union.

Historical Evolution of Polish Nationality Law

The legal architecture of Polish citizenship was born from the re‑emergence of the sovereign Polish state following the First World War. The modern framework commenced with the Citizenship Act of January 20, 1920, which served as the first consolidated statute for the Second Polish Republic.

The Act of 1920 was characterized by a restrictive approach toward dual nationality. Article 1 explicitly stated that a Polish citizen could not simultaneously be a citizen of another state. The subsequent Act of 1951 represented a dramatic shift, abolishing patriarchal restrictions and allowing children to inherit citizenship from their mothers.

The Military Paradox and Disqualification Nuances

Article 11 of the 1920 Act created a unique protection: men obliged to perform military service could only acquire foreign nationality after obtaining an exemption. Without such permission, they remained Polish citizens. Conversely, actual service in a foreign military without consent resulted in loss of citizenship—with the critical exception of Allied forces during WWII.
