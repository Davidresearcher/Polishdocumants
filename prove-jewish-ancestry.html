Proving Jewish Ancestry

Expert Document Recovery for Polish Citizenship and Genealogical Discovery

The Challenge of Post-War Archives

For many descendants of Polish Jews, the trail of family history often goes cold in 1939. The destruction of the Holocaust and the subsequent displacement of survivors left a "documentary void." However, a common misconception is that all records were destroyed.

In reality, millions of documents survived in regional Polish State Archives (Archiwum Państwowe) and local Registry Offices (USC). The challenge is not that the records don't exist—it's that they are hidden behind layers of **bureaucratic complexity, language barriers, and the passage of time.**

Why Proof of Ancestry is the Key

To acquire a Polish passport today, you do not "apply" for citizenship; you **confirm** that you already have it by descent. This requires proving an unbroken link to an ancestor who was a Polish citizen on or after 1918. For Jewish families, this often involves finding:

  • Army Records: Proof of service in the Polish Army (Wojsko Polskie) which confirms citizenship status.
  • Kahal (Community) Records: Jewish community registers that pre-date modern civil registries.
  • Residential Lists: "Księgi Ludności" (Books of Residents) which prove your ancestor actually lived in a specific Polish town.
  • Passport Applications: Original files from the 1920s and 30s that contain photographs and signatures of your ancestors.

Deciphering the "Lost" Languages

Depending on which part of Poland your ancestors came from, their records might not even be in Polish. Before 1918, Poland was partitioned between Russia, Germany, and Austria.

As a polyglot residing in both Israel and Poland, I manually search through handwritten records in Old Russian (Cyrillic), Yiddish, German, and Latin. Understanding the nuances of how Jewish surnames were recorded—and how they changed upon emigration—is the difference between a successful application and a rejection.
